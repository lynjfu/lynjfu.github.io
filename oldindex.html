About

I study innovation strategy — specifically, how firms can uncover new sources of value through their interactions with the wider innovation ecosystem. I utilize both computational simulations and qualitative archival methods in my work.

I previously worked in defence research, leading projects on elite combat performance. I also led a non-profit organization raising entrepreneurial intention in Singapore. I have a BSE in Mechanical Engineering from the University of Pennsylvania, and an MSE in Mechanical Engineering from Stanford University.

Research

Working papers:

  • Fu CJ "Setting the Stage: The Interplay of Firm Boundary and Learning at the Opera" (Job Market Paper)

    Vertical integration has traditionally been understood as the means by which firms can best experiment with different combinations of resources, so as to achieve valuable innovations in product architecture. However, the possibility of a disintegrated market to also provide valuable architectural innovations has not been well investigated. In this study, we see that if specialized firms in a disintegrated market are themselves searching for valuable product architectures to participate in, then the market can offer a multitude of architectural innovations that a given firm can leverage. This study examines this opportunity through the case of an opera company, which sought to integrate for the sake of architectural innovation, but ultimately disintegrated towards this end, despite their proven ability to integrate. This study utilizes both a qualitative archival analysis and a computational simulation to clarify the benefits that market-level search for architectural innovations can offer, and demonstrate how a firm can in fact undercut this value by attempting to integrate.
